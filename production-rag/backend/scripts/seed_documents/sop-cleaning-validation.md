# SOP-CLN-014: Cleaning Validation for Shared Manufacturing Equipment

## 1. Purpose
This procedure defines the requirements for validating cleaning processes on
product-contact equipment shared between multiple products. The goal is to
demonstrate that residues of the previous product, cleaning agents, and microbial
contamination are reduced to acceptable levels before the equipment is used for the
next product.

## 2. Scope
This SOP applies to all multi-product equipment in oral solid dosage and liquid
manufacturing areas. Dedicated equipment used for a single product is excluded but
must still follow routine cleaning under SOP-CLN-002.

## 3. Acceptance Criteria
Three independent acceptance limits are applied and the most stringent governs:
- **Dose-based limit:** carryover must not exceed 1/1000th of the minimum daily dose
  of the previous product in the maximum daily dose of the next product.
- **10 ppm limit:** no more than 10 parts per million of the previous product may
  appear in the next product.
- **Visually clean:** the equipment surface must show no visible residue under
  defined lighting.

Cleaning agent residues are limited based on the toxicological threshold of the
detergent, and the final rinse water must meet a total organic carbon limit of
500 ppb.

## 4. Sampling Methods
Two sampling techniques are used together:
- **Swab sampling** targets worst-case locations such as welds, gaskets, and hard to
  clean corners that are identified during equipment risk assessment.
- **Rinse sampling** evaluates the overall cleanliness of large surface areas and
  hard-to-swab geometries such as transfer pipework.

A minimum of three consecutive successful cleaning runs is required to consider a
cleaning process validated. Recovery studies must establish swab recovery factors
before validation sampling begins.

## 5. Worst-Case Product Bracketing
A worst-case product is selected for validation based on solubility, toxicity, and
difficulty of cleaning. Validation of the worst-case product supports a family of
similar products manufactured on the same equipment train, reducing the number of
validation studies required.

## 6. Revalidation
Cleaning validation must be reviewed when a new product is introduced, when the
cleaning procedure changes, when equipment is modified, or at a maximum interval of
three years. A change to the analytical method used to detect residues also triggers
a review.
